Welcome

Welcome to the hands-on lab! This is where you get to roll up your sleeves and experience Microsoft 365 Copilot firsthand. Everything you have learned so far comes together here. You will work with your own data, experiment with real prompts, and discover what Copilot can actually do for your day-to-day work.

There are two challenges today. The first is a fast-paced, creative warm-up. The second is a guided scenario that puts your new skills to work on something practical.

Challenge 1: Jarvis for Work

A team prompt-creation challenge across 4 categories. Create your best prompts, submit via Forms, and let AI judge the winners. Points up for grabs!

Challenge 2: The Copilot Champion

Work in pairs through a scenario that takes you from research to content creation, data analysis, and building a toolkit for your team.

Rules of Engagement

  • Work together. Share ideas, help each other, and have fun.
  • Use your real work data where applicable. Copilot works best with your actual emails, files, and calendar.
  • Experiment with prompts. The first prompt is a starting point. Refine it, add context, and see how results improve.
  • There are no wrong answers. The goal is to discover what Copilot can do for you.
Jarvis for Work Challenge

Time to put your creativity to the test. In this challenge, each team will create their best Copilot prompts across 4 categories. Each category is worth 25 points, for a total of 100 points. The team with the highest overall score wins.

How it works:
  1. Explore the 4 categories below. Each has a theme and example prompts for inspiration.
  2. Create your team's best prompt for each category (~20 min). You can split up (one person per category) or work together.
  3. Submit your prompts via the Microsoft Forms link below (~5 min).

The 4 Categories

Create one prompt per category. Use the examples below for inspiration, but the best entries will be original prompts that go beyond these.

Most Useful (25 pts)

Daily drivers that replace manual work. Prompts you would actually use every day.

Example prompts for inspiration:
  • The Radar -- What needs my attention today?
  • The Backstory -- Catch me up on what I missed
  • The Waiting-On-Me List -- What am I blocking?
Here's one to get you started: Monday Morning Briefing
Scan my emails, Teams chats, and meetings from the past 24 hours. Organize into: URGENT (act today) -- what needs immediate response and who is waiting on me; IMPORTANT (act this week) -- decisions made, tasks assigned to me, threads needing my input; FYI ONLY -- one line each, no action needed; REPLIES I OWE -- every message waiting for my response, in order of urgency, with a draft reply for each. Get me fully operational in under 5 minutes.

Most Creative (25 pts)

Reveal hidden insights in your data. Prompts that make you see your own work differently.

Example prompts for inspiration:
  • Personality Profiler -- What does my communication style say about me?
  • Relationship Mapper -- Who do I collaborate with most?
  • Writing Mirror -- What patterns show up in my writing?
Here's one to get you started: The Crystal Ball
Based on my emails, chats, and meetings from the past 2 weeks, identify 3 things that are likely to become problems or escalations in the next 7 days. Look for patterns like: topics that keep resurfacing without resolution, deadlines people are getting nervous about, threads where the tone is getting tense, commitments at risk of being missed, or people who have gone unusually quiet. For each risk, explain the early warning signs and suggest one preventive action I can take today.

Most Fun (25 pts)

Entertaining yet surprisingly accurate insights. Prompts that make everyone laugh and think.

Example prompts for inspiration:
  • Meeting Verdict -- Was that meeting actually worth attending?
  • Humble Brag Generator -- Turn my week into a LinkedIn post
  • Work Wrapped -- My year in review, Spotify style
Here's one to get you started: Calendar Health Report
Look at my calendar for the past 2 weeks and produce a Calendar Health Report formatted like a doctor's diagnosis. Include: a Symptoms section (back-to-back meetings, vague meeting titles like "Sync" or "Catch-up"), a Test Results section (hours in meetings vs. focus time), a Diagnosis, and a Prescription with 3 recommendations. End with a Calendar Health Score out of 10. Keep the tone light and the language accessible.

Most Time-Saving (25 pts)

Compress hours into minutes. Prompts that automate the tedious parts of work.

Example prompts for inspiration:
  • Follow-Up Machine -- Draft follow-ups for all my open threads
  • Status Generator -- Create my weekly status update
  • End-of-Week Wrap -- Summarize everything I did this week
Here's one to get you started: The PTO Rescue
I have been out of office for the past few days. Search all my emails, Teams chats, and meeting transcripts from while I was away. Give me: URGENT (act today) -- what needs immediate response and who is waiting; IMPORTANT (act this week) -- decisions made, tasks assigned, threads needing my input; FYI ONLY -- one line each; MEETINGS I MISSED -- 2-sentence summary of each with my action items; REPLIES I NEED TO SEND -- every message waiting for my response, with a draft reply for each. Get me caught up in 10 minutes.
Tips for Writing Great Prompts
  • Be specific about what you want. "Summarize my emails" is okay. "Summarize emails from my manager this week about the product launch, grouped by topic" is better.
  • Tell Copilot the format. Want bullet points? A table? A 3-sentence summary? Say so.
  • Give it a role. "As my executive assistant, review my calendar for next week and flag any conflicts or preparation I need to do."
  • Set constraints. "Keep it under 200 words." "Focus only on action items." "Exclude anything before March."
  • Iterate. Your first prompt rarely gives the perfect result. Refine based on what comes back.

The Copilot Champion

Duration: ~60 minutes | Work in pairs

You have just been appointed as the Copilot Champion for your department. Your manager expects you to lead the team's AI adoption, but honestly, you are still figuring it out yourself. You have one week before your first team presentation. Time to use Copilot to become the champion your team needs.

Your mission: Work through 4 challenges that follow a natural progression -- from getting oriented, to preparing your team, to understanding their needs, to equipping them for success. Each challenge builds on the last.

Challenge 1

Know Your Mission Warm-up

Habits: MH4 Research Assistant Copilot Chat (Web)
You have just been named your department's Copilot Champion. Congratulations! But what does that actually mean? Before you can lead anyone, you need to understand the role, what success looks like, and what resources are available. Time to do some research using publicly available materials.
Search the web for information about what a Microsoft 365 Copilot Champion does. Focus on official Microsoft resources, the Microsoft Copilot adoption playbook, and reputable sources. Summarize your findings into a brief overview covering: (1) what a Copilot Champion is expected to do and their typical responsibilities, (2) any recommended goals or success metrics for driving AI adoption, (3) resources, communities, or training paths available for Copilot Champions. Keep the summary practical and actionable.
Try following up with more specific questions like "What are common challenges Copilot Champions face in the first 30 days?" or "What does a typical Copilot adoption timeline look like?" Refine your search by asking for specific frameworks or templates.
What did you learn about the Copilot Champion role? Were there resources or communities you did not know existed? How could this web research approach save time when starting any new initiative?
Challenge 2

Rally the Troops Core

Habits: MH5 Content Creator Copilot in Word Copilot in PowerPoint
Your department head has asked you to present at next week's team meeting. The goal: get your colleagues excited about Copilot and help them understand what it can do for their specific work. You need a briefing that is clear, relevant, and not just another corporate slide deck.
Step 1: Create the outline in Word

Open a new Word document and use Copilot in Word to draft an outline for your team briefing. Review the outline, refine it, and make sure it covers the key points your team needs to hear.

Draft a structured outline for a 10-minute team briefing about Microsoft 365 Copilot. Include these sections: (1) a simple explanation of what Copilot is and how it works, (2) three specific examples of how it can help with common tasks, (3) a brief FAQ covering the top concerns people usually have about AI at work (privacy, accuracy, job impact), and (4) a clear next step for the team to try. Keep the tone conversational and practical, not salesy.
Step 2: Create the presentation in PowerPoint

Once you are happy with your Word document, open PowerPoint and use Copilot in PowerPoint to create a presentation from the document you just created.

Create a presentation from [/file]. Keep it concise with clear visuals, one key message per slide, and a professional but approachable design. Aim for 6-8 slides.
After generating the presentation, try asking Copilot to "Add speaker notes to all slides." You can also ask it to add an agenda slide or a closing slide with next steps.
How close was the first draft to something you would actually present? What was the difference between starting in Word versus going straight to PowerPoint? How many rounds of refinement did it take?
Challenge 3

Read the Room Core

Habits: MH7 Data Analyst Copilot Chat (Web or Work)
Before your presentation, you decided to run a quick pulse check. You sent your colleagues a short survey asking about their biggest time sinks, comfort level with AI, and concerns about using AI at work. The responses are in. Now you need to make sense of them.

Download the survey data: Survey-Data.xlsx

Open Copilot Chat and upload the Excel file. Then use the prompt below to analyze the results.

Analyze the attached survey data about our team's AI readiness. Give me: (1) the top 3 biggest time sinks reported by the team, (2) an overall comfort level with AI tools -- group respondents into "enthusiastic," "curious but cautious," and "skeptical," (3) the most common concerns about AI at work, and (4) specific recommendations for how I should tailor my Copilot introduction based on these findings. Present the analysis as a brief summary I can reference during my presentation, with key statistics highlighted.
Try asking follow-up questions like "Create a chart showing the distribution of comfort levels" or "Which department has the highest AI readiness?" You can also ask Copilot to cross-reference the time sinks with the comfort levels to find your best early adopters.
How well did Copilot identify patterns in the data? Did the recommendations feel actionable? How would data-driven insights like these change how you approach team communication?
Challenge 4

The Champion's Toolkit Stretch

Habits: MH4 Research Assistant MH6 Document Insights Copilot Chat (Web) Researcher Agent
The presentation went well and your colleagues are interested. Now they want something practical -- a quick-start guide they can use on day one. Your job is to pull together a "Getting Started with Copilot" toolkit: concise, skimmable, and immediately useful. This is your most complex challenge yet -- it combines deep research with the ability to distill information into something anyone can pick up and use.
Step 1: Deep research

Use Copilot Chat (Web) to conduct a thorough research on Microsoft 365 Copilot best practices, tips, and getting-started guidance from official and reputable sources. Save the research report as a Word document.

Conduct a deep research on getting started with Microsoft 365 Copilot. Target audience: general busy business professionals. Search for official Microsoft documentation, adoption guides, best practices, and expert recommendations. Cover: (1) what Copilot can do across Chat and Microsoft 365 apps, (2) the most effective prompting techniques and common mistakes to avoid, (3) privacy and data handling -- what Copilot can and cannot access, (4) real-world success stories or use cases from organizations that have adopted Copilot, and (5) recommended first-week activities for new Copilot users. Compile everything into a comprehensive research report with sources cited.
Step 2: Create the guide

Using the research report you just saved, ask Copilot to distill it into a practical, one-page quick-start guide for your team.

Using the research report I just created, distill the key information into a practical "Getting Started with Copilot" guide for my team. Structure it as: (1) "What is Copilot?" -- a 2-sentence explanation anyone can understand, (2) "5 Things to Try on Day One" -- specific prompts for common tasks like email triage, meeting prep, and document drafting, (3) "FAQ" -- answers to the top 5 questions people ask about Copilot, and (4) "Pro Tips" -- 3 advanced techniques for getting more out of Copilot. Keep the language simple, avoid jargon, and make it skimmable. This should fit on one page.
This is a two-step challenge by design. The research report gives Copilot a rich knowledge base to draw from, and the guide prompt asks it to distill and reformat. Compare the guide to your research report -- notice how the distillation step creates something much more usable. Try asking Copilot to create an FAQ section based on the concerns from your earlier survey analysis.
How did you break this complex task into smaller prompts? What was the difference between the research report and the final guide? Would your team actually use this guide?

Key Takeaways
  • Start with what you know. The best prompts come from your own work. The more specific and grounded your prompt is, the better the result.
  • Iterate, don't accept. Your first prompt is a draft. Refine it based on what Copilot gives you back. Add constraints, change the format, narrow the scope.
  • Make it a habit. The prompts that save the most time are the ones you use repeatedly. Find your daily drivers and build them into your routine.
  • Share what works. When you find a great prompt, share it with your team. One person's discovery can save everyone time.
